Vipassana

10-day Courses FAQ

  • Food

    What do you eat at Vipassana?

    The food was typically european (please consider that this was at the vipassana center in Germany). Or shall we say a vegetarian version of ordinary european food. For example: Jacket potatoes, roasted veggies, soups, pasta. The food seemed to be vegan with dairy products on the side (like cheese and sauce). We were also served some very mildly spiced tofu and curry dishes. Overall there were always lots of side dishes and toppings and bread you could pimp your food with. For breakfast they served porridge (oats) and different bread with lots of different spreads. You only eat two meals per day, and it was really easy to get adjused to the lack of dinner. Anyway, intermittent fasting is good for you, right? The aquintance who took the course in India, said the food was typically Indian.

  • Clothes

    What should I wear at the vipassana course?

    Sweatpants (leggings were not allowed). Something comfy. Warm socks and slippers.

  • Items

    What should I bring?

    At the meditation hall you can borrow blankets and cushions. I had read somewhere about blankets, so I thought I was clever to bring one, however that was not necessary since you could borrow there. The space in my suitcase would have used more efficiently if I had packed more clothes, I kept thinking, before I tried to have no craving to the clothes still back home. If you have some special cushions or meditation bench you could bring them. The information we got was that we should bring a torch. However I (N) forgot mine, and in the end I wasn't missing it. The pathways were all illuminated. We were also asked to bring an alarm clock. Cellphones were not allowed. I found it rather difficult to find one in the brick and mortar stores on the last minute... Misstake number one... Misstake number two was that I didn't read the instructions before hand so when I got there I did not know how to use the alarm function. Since I was recovering from a cold and generally need to sleep alot the breaks were often spent napping. I would have been more at ease if I knew how to set the alarm. In the end I didn't oversleep, though.

  • Sitting

    How do you sit?

    At the meditation hall there were lots of different cushions. It might be an advantage if you have some sort of idea how to use them, or how you sit. I had no clue, people rushed to the cupboard with all the stuff and grabbed what they needed. I on the other hand had on the first day no idea of how and what to use. You can of course move (atleast the first days).
    For people with special needs it was also possible to get chairs to sit on.

  • Hippies and Hipsters

    What kind of people will be at the center?

    Mixed. Normal people. Both hippies and hipsters. A lot of people seemed to have been at some kind of turning point in their lives. Changing careers and jobs. From beeing employees to going solo. Were in the process of finding out what kind of job they wanted to do. Or going back to school. I found it all very interesting and exciting. So many brave people!

  • Difficulties

    Will I make it?

    Hardly any one seems to leave the course in advance. And it is not really supported. Before the course I had real concerns about the one hour sessions where you are not suppose to move. I even thought that you would be thrown out of the course if you moved. That was not the case, and in the end I even kind of prefered those sessions. Overall I found the course both more difficult and easier than I had imagined.

  • Outcomes

    Will I be a new person afterwards?

    Both yes and no. I felt that everything had changed afterwards. However I still have the same difficulties, just less so. I am still hottempered and anxious. My outlook on everything have changed.

  • Schedule

    Will I get lost?

    One of my mundane concerns was that I might get lost, and/ or don't know where I am supposed to go to since one can't ask. (I have bad sense of direction)
    All over the center there were timetables aswell as watches so it was quite evident what was happening next. There were also not that many places you go to so it was very easy to learn were to go.

  • No-speaking

    Is it difficult to not speak?

    For me no. One of my most talkative acquaintances who had also taken a course also said when I asked him "not at all".

  • Rooms

    How are the living quarters?

    In Triebel there were shared rooms for both beginners (those taking a course for the first time) and old students There were also single rooms. People with physical problems as well as old people got the single rooms. Personally I was relived to have a shared accomondation, since I thought I might feel lonely if staying all by myself. Even if I wasn't allowed to speak to anyone (and really you should not even look at the others) I liked having people around. We were four people in one room and several rooms shared a bathroom with several toilets. There were also a seperate shower room. Overall I had a flashback from summer camp, the housing reminded me of that.

  • Service

    Will I have to do any work?

    The courses are all voluntary based and on the last day we had to clean our room as well do some house cleaning. We filled out a form what our prefered task were and we were assigned tasks.

  • Cost

    Is it really free?

    After the course you are asked to make a donation if the course was helpful for you. The donation is that you pay for another student so to speak. It was not checked in anyway how much you donated. They had bank slipsfor differen t centers you could take with you as well as a table with a cardreader if you wanted to pay directly there. There were neither a pressure on how much to donate nor was there a followup on how much you donated.
